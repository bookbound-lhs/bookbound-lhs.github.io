Simon vs The Homo Sapiens Agenda: Book Review
by: Jan Loren G. Tugay

Simon, a 17-year-old, third-year high schooler, is living a perfect life. He has a very close relationship with his two sisters, loving parents, and friends Simon has known since forever, Nick and Leah, and Abby, a transferee but quickly became his confidant. But there is a problem. He is gay, and is closeted, for the fear of things changing for the worst, so he never really planned to come out. However, upon knowing that there is another gay student in their school, with a codename Blue, he got curious of the mystery person’s identity, and with a few exchanged emails, he develops feelings for Blue. Although the two became really close, Blue still does not feel safe on letting go of his anonymity, despite the story taking place in Georgia, an openly-gay capital state in the US. But when Martin, a nerd who has a liking for Simon’s friend Abby, saw the exchanged emails of the two, he purposely used it as blackmail for Simon to help him get closer to Abby. Stuck into letting go of his secret or helping a weirdo, he picked the latter and eventually the two got close. However, this still left Simon’s world unbalanced, losing his first true love, or knowing the true identity of who is behind the email address.

The development of Simon and Blue’s relationship is very much adorable, but despite the strategic location which is an open-minded state in the US, they are still reminded of the fact that same-sex relationships are still not widely accepted. Also, the way the characters handled internal drama is mature and against norms. With the typical teenager’s moods and characteristics, the characters went far from the stereotypical.

The portrayal of family relationships in the book is very typical and even though Simon’s family is deemed as perfect, it still featured the weak points of a family, and how they overcame and managed through it. Same as with his relationship to his friends, it depicted a norm that we cannot know everything about everyone, and sometimes things are better kept for ourselves.  The book also managed to show intimacy without the sexual contact, which is commonly deemed as the typical ending of most young adult novels, but with this book, the characters displayed affection minus the physical contact and still proved that their love is true. 

Simon vs. The Homo Sapiens Agenda is a unique novel that went against the norms of young adult titles. It showed the perfection of an imperfect life of a gay teenager, and how he tried to overcome the odds and handled problems differently but with ease. It introduced a diverse storyline that tackled a mature issue but still offered an entertainment that still relates and speaks to the audience.
