Into the Water: Book Review
by: Ma. Jullienne B. De Castro

With a standpoint of crime fiction, the writer established a review for the literary work of Paula Hawkins with regards to the success of the past bestselling book, The Girl on the Train that unintentionally set the standard for the appraisal of Into the Water. The book failed to satisfy the crime readers with the same exuberant experience as with The Girl on the Train, only emphasizing the memoir of women in the story.

 As J.D. Barker perceptively states that, “Into the Water has a fascinating quasi-historical fiction streak that runs through it, and the book seemed to find its footing best when Hawkins delves into this storyline.” Diving deep into the storyline, a woman named Nel Abott, who conducted a research driven by curiosity about the mysterious water, was found dead at the pool where troublesome women through past generations have died. Hawkins furnished a deep connection with the readers through the mysterious and supernatural relation between the women of the water.

 The writer points out that the whole story lacks at the “psychological thriller” outlook and merely implying intellect feelings rather than relating to deep inward feelings for most readers. The book did not meet the expectations of the writer as he search for a mystery-based plot of Hawkins’s previous book. Barker has drawn attention to the fact that this book will not be suitable for the readers who are engrossed with exploring mystery and crime.
